The Bystander Initiative

 

Everyone has a role in sexual assault prevention. Be a proactive bystander. 

 

The University of Windsor offers sexual assault prevention training through student-led workshops known as Bringing in the Bystander. The workshop encourages participants to envision themselves as proactive bystanders that have the opportunity to intervene and stop sexual assault before it happens. 

 

Bringing in the Bystander is a three-hour-long workshop that speaks directly to the experiences faced by University of Windsor students. These workshops take an interactive and intersectional approach to sexual assault prevention. Their purpose is to train students in prevention measures, in the hope of dismantling rape culture in the campus community. Additionally, the program teaches students transferable intervention skills. Students can apply these skills in various situations such as intervening in anti-Black, homophobic or transphobic scenarios. 

 

The workshops are part of the broader program, the Bystander Initiative; the initiative takes a campus-wide approach to spread awareness on sexual assault prevention measures. It incorporates student-led workshops and faculty-led courses. 

 

The Bystander Initiative offers two courses that teach students how to lead the workshops: Practical Strategies for Social Change (3500) and Practicum in Social Change (4500). The courses can be taken as a credit towards Criminology, General Social Science, Psychology, Social Work, among others. In its inception, the courses were focused on students from the humanities. However, over time it has become a campus-wide program. 

 

The general student consensus is that both the workshop and courses are an educational and enjoyable experience. Michael Mastrogiacomo, a fourth-year Aeronautics student, expressed his admiration for the program, “ I have fallen in love with the program because it raises community awareness of the seriousness of this issue and provides individuals with the tools they need to positively intervene when they witness any type of harmful behaviour.” Mastrogiacomo is currently taking the 4500-level and will be facilitating workshops. 

 

Due to the nature of the workshop content, it is understandable that students might be hesitant to participate. The workshop emphasises respect and self-care, actively encouraging students to prioritise their well-being. Students often leave the workshop pleasantly surprised by how well the content is handled. Yasmeena Haidar-Ahmed, a sociology student, commented that “as someone who’s had experiences, I’m often skeptical of workshops. The Bystander Initiative is clearly run by individuals who understand the struggle and took steps to teach empathy without the “trauma porn.” They checked on participants and ensured that it was a safe space to engage and learn. It provides an element of trust in the campus community knowing that preventative measures are being taken seriously.

 

The coordinator of the Bystander Initiative, Dr. Frances Cachon, encourages student feedback. The program is responsive to community feedback and is actively looking for ways to improve its workshops and courses. 

 

The Bystander Initiative is a great way to involve oneself in the community. The program offers genuine insight into sexual assault prevention and gives students the tools necessary to create change.
